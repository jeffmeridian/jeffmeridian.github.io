Serial Number One. #

Thorne's fingers danced across the cool surface of the slab, tracing patterns where once human skin had lain still. He froze, eyes snapping up to meet those of his partner, Elianore, who stood at the periphery of the makeshift scene. The flickering fluorescent lights above seemed to mock Thorne, casting an unhealthy glow over the macabre display.

"Find anything?" Thorne asked, voice low and hesitant.

Elianore stepped forward, her usually stoic expression a mask. "Nothing out of the ordinary... yet."

Thorne's gaze returned to the body, his eyes tracing the line where neck met shoulder. The angle was off, as if the victim had been turned away from them with an unspoken plea for help. Thorne knelt closer, fingers grazing the torn fabric of a black jacket.

He inhaled, the scent of rain and decay wafting up. The smell told him little, though – it spoke more to Thorne's own melancholy mood than the victim's presence. Rain washed away footprints, making it as easy for a hitman to appear or disappear as a ghost.

The eyes caught his attention anew. A glint of silver peeked from beneath the jacket, accompanied by a faint outline of something else - a tattoo, or possibly the last gasp of desperation – in stark black ink.

With calculated hands Thorne pulled at the fabric now, trying not to push too hard for fear of disrupting DNA evidence. Finally, the edges lifted free, and he dropped them to the ground with practiced stealth.

The air that followed was heavy - static charged anticipation – as Thorne reached down to remove the object caught beneath. A silver necklace shone under fluorescent light, chain frayed in its current state. Something more profound seemed lost amidst the debris. Thorne turned it over between his fingers.

The symbol on the pendant seemed ancient. The image was an inverted triangle, with symbols that felt like something from forbidden texts. The metal itself bore the weight of history – something from a bygone era or perhaps too much knowledge for any one mortal lifespan to handle.

"You never see this before," Thorne mused aloud, as if it required affirmation rather than explanation.

"That's what worries you?" Elianore asked, her expression detached but probing.

Thorne did not have an answer. All he could say was: "What does it mean?"

The silence that followed stretched like brittle vines against his skin – a stark reminder of the unknown.. The fluorescent lights overhead cast a sickly sheen over Elianore's face, but her eyes remained unflinching as she approached Thorne. She crouched beside him, surveying the discarded fabric and the glint of silver on the ground. A shiver ran down her spine with each whisper-quiet step.

Thorne's fingers continued to cradle the pendant, his expression a study in rancid curiosity. "It looks old," he said aloud, as if daring the artifact to contradict him.

"You'd think so," Elianore replied, her attention snapping back to the necklace, "but what's truly unsettling is how... contained it feels." The word itself whispered softly into the air, and she reached out with a gloved hand to gently pry it free. In doing so, she almost hesitated before touching the cool metal.

The chain, no longer secured by whatever had tied it down, dangled at her fingertips like an abandoned thread from the fabric of reality. Elianore felt small in comparison, the tattered remains of the victim's jacket seeming to mock her with their frayed ends and tangled paths. Even under fluorescent light, the pendant shone brighter now, drawing the focus inward on Thorne.

"You really don't see this?" Elianore asked once more.

Thorne didn't lift his gaze from his grasp on the pendant, only turned it slightly by means of the subtle turn of his fingers against the clasp. The symbol beneath remained a labyrinth of unutterable meaning to him - so much like being an empty canvas that was supposed to tell a story but yielded nothing upon inspection.

The tension in Thorne's body released once his thumb found purchase on the chain loop, pulling down in short, smooth pulls until they slid from his fingers. In this slow progression, he allowed her into a position just before she understood exactly where we are headed: all questions are not as they initially seem.

"Let's be honest," Elianore said softly now, with calculated patience on the surface. "This isn't something to be found anywhere or anything that appears out of nowhere—"

Thorne finally turned their eyes to meet hers for a moment before continuing. The air left them felt like he pulled in the very breath right back out.

"All signs point otherwise," Thorne continued aloud, moving past Elianore's words with a deliberate slowness she hadn't witnessed from him yet. "It looks almost deliberately hidden. The fabric on all sides appears nearly torn away—"

A pause. Only the gentle lapping of rainwater on pavement interrupted Elianore, then the distant murmur of people passing by, their footsteps blending softly into the shadows.

Thorne reached deep at heart for all unspoken words he hadn't uttered yet. 'You can touch it,' he said quietly, without turning back to her once but his expression seemed to convey, "Please don't leave.". Thorne navigated the alleys of Nexus Inc., the urban sprawl's neon-drenched facades slicing through his senses like shards of broken glass. Every step he took echoed with a sense of foreboding, the city itself seemed to be watching him with calculating eyes, weighing its next move. He dodged pedestrians and market stalls, their colorful wares threatening to entangle his path as he sprinted toward the Nexus Archives.

Inside, rows upon rows of dusty files and flickering holographic displays lined the shelves, casting a dimly lit glow over Thorne's determined strides. His thoughts danced on the periphery of consciousness like restless shadows, avoiding scrutiny even as they sought to be coaxed into awareness.

Within the archive's central hub, an information stream flowed freely from one computer terminal to another, their screens flickering in syncopated rhythm with Thorne's own heartbeat. Among them were countless lines of code and schematics that only revealed a smattering glimpse at the elusive answer he hunted – hidden behind layers upon layers.

Amidst hushed hums and muffled clicks, Thorne knelt before an information feed from the late Dr. Eliza Lee, rumored last known investigator in synthetic life research – she'd gone underground weeks ago with hints of her disappearance spreading quietly along underground forums.

"This could be it," he stated aloud to himself as his fingers hovered hesitantly above a keyboard key bound for encryption-activated protocols.

A chill passed down Thorne's spine. Something was amiss beneath the facade - that particular unease that stirred even before a full realization began – echoed by countless digital messages hinting at synthetic past experiments gone haywire.

Taking a deep breath to steel himself, he began downloading a single file labeled "Project Luminance".

Digital files began loading down his devices with rhythmic beeps and flashes of blue, echoing the steady, hollow beat Thorne sought - but nothing appeared as he delved deeper into data records – fragmented reports that contained hints about this elusive key.

Suddenly an information stream broke free from behind static screens for a fleeting instant. Screens flickered brighter before disappearing immediately – one second later – 'Confidentiality Level: Critical,' echoed back once more, prompting Thorne's instincts to speed up rather than proceed calmly.... Thorne's gaze faltered as he confronted the blinding radiance of the information stream, now amplified to an otherworldly intensity that seemed to pierce through every layer of his consciousness. Screens exploded into kaleidoscopic bursts, a dazzling dance of colors and textures that wrestled for dominance in his vision. Digital shards hurtled towards him with pinpoint precision, their fragmented reports coalescing into disjointed phrases like forgotten prayers being whispered to an unseen deity.

Fingers drummed against his thighs in rhythmic synchrony, as if an unconscious heartbeat was demanding clarity amidst the cacophony of data points swirling around him. A shiver coursed through his arms, evoking a sudden sense of frost-bitten fingers, even though the air surrounding him had been warm and oppressive just moments before.

The room's ambient scent – stale air from years of compressed documents, acrid tangs of burning circuit modules that now seemed to dance within the upper atmosphere – was momentarily swept aside as Thorne's ears worked the digital feedback loop, tuning his sensitivity towards an elusive frequency hidden below this turbulent surface. And it was there that he finally grasped the significance: what Dr. Lee had unearthed.

Thorne stood, striding purposefully toward a far corner of the room, where an unassuming console waited like a trusted ally in the digital dark. The file labeled Project Luminance rested on his work station – for an instant. As soon as he reached out to touch it with an expectant finger, the computer terminal froze upon him with crystal clarity; then in that single fractured second before rebooting into safe protocols, Dr. Lee's cryptic words came back into focus – 'Synthetic lives are ephemeral, yet truth is hidden within shadows.'

The digital world momentarily held its breath as he stood frozen and unsure over the data feed, his eyes brimming with questions for a long-overdue clarity. He raised an almost desperate whisper at the empty air: 'Why...and what have we truly lost?'

And nothing but static filled the silence which followed, like a solemn acknowledgement that Thorne had finally understood too much..

The Handler. #

Thorne's eyes snapped open, and for an instant, he was back on the rooftop, the city streets his canvas as he sprinted towards freedom. The familiar rush of adrenaline coursed through his veins like a high-octane fuel that rebooted his senses. He landed hard onto the concrete floor of his small apartment, the crash leaving dents in air molecules and shattering any remaining illusion of a peaceful night's sleep.

The room around him – dingy fluorescent lights overhead, peeling paint on walls, and the acrid smell of old electronics huddled together like a toxic bouquet – seemed to writhe away the warmth from his skin. Thorne rolled onto his back, arms splayed wide as if anchoring himself against an invisible storm. His vision blazed in shards: computer screens flashing like fireflies in synchronization with his racing heart, images superimposed on top of themselves, screaming for attention. He was a sleepwalker trapped in a dreamless nightmare.

Panting heavily, Thorne slowly slid down the wall until he rested against the cold baseboard, trying to untangle his frayed thoughts while fighting the creeping sense that something had come to fetch him tonight. It wasn't a question of 'when,' but more so of why – every time these nocturnal wake-up calls struck, there was always this peculiar feeling that their meaning was tied to something greater than himself.

Breathing in synchrony with his labored heart rate became an exercise in finding order amidst disarray. The first thing noticed on his breathing wasn't his breath: there would be no air – only the faint, tingling echoes of a sigh he hadn't made – until a minute or so later when it materialized as if Thorne's heart had become the very vessel that sustained him.

There was a knock, tentative at best, then growing to a hesitant thunder. If one could categorize fear by its physical presence in this room alone, they might pinpoint the sound to 'thud - tap - knock'; something disheveled yet methodical as an individual attempting not to alert their internal warning system – which would still manage to trigger all of its default panic responses within a mere heartbeat. It came again, less confidently this time, a series of hesitant thuds and clicks that formed more words than actions: 'K-knock', it started softly enough; Thorne had seen enough instances like this.

With each incremental tap, the urgency beneath those hesitant knocks became clearer. Thorne rolled to his feet with surprising ease, still trying to connect a sequence of sounds into language that made sense. He blinked multiple times at an almost impossibly dim lighting in his place before understanding finally seeped its way past what looked like brain fog. If this wasn't something designed to wake the unawake and shake loose – as well as bring someone down by any means necessary – how else could Thorne know exactly when he was on his own territory?

"Who is it?" Thorne barked out in time with the knocking growing louder. It made a faint crack before subsiding momentarily; what felt like heavy boots scraping against leather outside.

"No one," came the unreplicated response; each sound echoing back and forth in dissonant echoes. The door opened, sunlight spilling upon his weathered skin to highlight each deep scratch – wounds he'd accumulated during one of fewer a multitude of covert operations for an organization unknown even to him at that moment.

Thorne hesitated before letting out some measured words; what little time left was dwindling fast and in exchange for not getting any help soon. "Let just you come inside."

Beyond the now-open door existed darkness illuminated as if sunlight seeped from everywhere but within no walls of this shabby flat stood an imposing figure, its mass obscured by a well-worn trench coat slumped over to one side, the shoulder barely parting that awkwardly hung bulk.

The individual slowly unfolded steps toward Thorne before sliding right into the spot where Thorne was supposed to be – his unsteady frame unwavering despite visible discomfort in every joint. "It's time," they said with all possible finality: "the operation begins now."

Thorne blinked a moment harder than necessary and asked a single question while staring for an eternity at the unknown person looming before him, silhouetted like black marble against flickering fluorescent lights – "What've we done?". The fluorescent lights above flickered with a jerky rhythm, spilling dim light across Agent Kael's austere profile as she loomed in the doorway. Her imposing frame seemed to fill the entire space, eclipsing the once-shabby flat that now looked almost meek by comparison. Thorne's weary eyes struggled to adjust, narrowing against the brief pain of too much light.

"You're not... you," Thorne stammered, mind racing with every conflicting memory and fragmentary recollection that refused to coalesce into a coherent whole. Each crack in his mental landscape was like an invisible fissure spreading across a glassy surface – shattering at any moment with the slightest pressure.

"I'm sorry about that," Agent Kael's voice said from just beyond his ear, like she'd stepped out of the shadows themselves. A crisp precision to her words didn't fit with this haphazard jumble of memories and mangled reality that somehow seemed to still hold together – almost despite itself. That was probably more important than ever right now.

Kael slid a gaze in every direction as if double-checking she'd entered the correct frame, giving an almost imperceptible lean before finally confronting Thorne head-on, her whole being coiled back straight and unyielding. Those piercing blue eyes locked onto his face with calculated intensity that made him tighten around each knotted muscle of unease – just so.

The air inside seemed to thicken as the seconds dragged out in heavy drag, like dust accumulating on skin that ached under every inch of unwanted intrusion. It stung when it settled behind her ears. He'd noticed, more often than he cared to recall.

"Agent Kael," Thorne croaked into this growing discomfort, voice caught between rough gravel and the soft lopsided desperation rising in his mind like a dying fire – "how did you get here?"

Kael flicked her gaze away momentarily before stepping directly forward as if there was no choice on her part. It landed just behind him when she walked and still Thorne knew this space wasn't designed with anyone else's presence – this had always been for one person, or a particular version of it.

"I'm your stability officer," she said in slow statement that pulled at the frayed edges of his mind but did something else as well. Something he could feel more strongly: unease growing inside and spreading through every nerve like dark smoke filling lungs – this was going somewhere only one direction, no turning back until Thorne had stepped off into it.

"Removal," Kael continued in that careful measured tone; slow and deliberate to the point of unassailable clarity, just as her overall demeanor conveyed complete confidence it couldn't fail. "That's a high category because you were placed under extreme risk during a particularly volatile operation," she gave back his question – Thorne blinked harder at this realization like he could possibly reassemble these mangled things into order on the fly – "I removed you."

Every instinct screamed that something vital had been knocked from beneath him, nothing returning immediately and making his groundless footing seem all right for once. Panic was like a rising tide that wouldn't get swallowed by quick shallow breathing.

"What do you mean 'removed'?" Thorne asked again now through gritted teeth; he took that step forward as Kael's words settled – the steps felt more measured with time passing but still he could feel an almost imperceptible hesitation before her response came. There was no time to correct this or recover from it once said – every moment mattered in places Thorne had come to trust by now.

"The memories, I placed new ones," Kael revealed her next piece of information a little too quickly though she controlled each word as delicately as she could; eyes not taking any pause over adjusting light levels – the way she made sure everything worked under and with one other was uncanny. This was someone used to control every detail that surrounded their people's perception, leaving no space open for her adversaries.

Her explanation cut through Thorne like a straight blade across jagged metal – it wasn't pain he felt so much as disremembering his life in an instant because everything had changed in the split second – what used to be familiar recollections and memories just vanished out of him, all those moments being replaced by nothing more than a shallow echo of who he'd once been for no reason whatsoever.

It shook something deep in him. He stepped against his will when this realization took hold so he faced out at Kael.

"The operation has begun," her voice was as low and even now as the sound of running water but the cold dread spreading through Thorne's veins from what had unfolded made every hair on his skin twitch – if anything he could still sense within any shred of time left for reaction. She spoke like she understood an invisible word shared only between them.

A fleeting uncertainty slid in behind Kael's unwavering confidence before her gaze shifted toward the space beyond this room, flickered towards the wall of windows that needed washing and offered little beyond – another momentary break from being fully focused on Thorne — though he suspected every single thread from their lives was now tied by unseen means, waiting to be pulled at his choice.

Thorne blinked at each step forward toward some hidden future Kael spoke without voice but felt like a whispered name that hung over the edge of awareness – and slowly it took shape with one slow glance. Before him stood in flickering light the outline he needed most desperately: himself.

One face turned back to match Thorne's eyes, face no more distinct than her other features because everything was blended at this point; for just a moment he tried every attempt to differentiate anything in their shared skin – skin where memories lay. Those moments lingered deep down without his knowing before shattering the fragile balance with one simple question:. Thorne's eyes narrowed, his gaze burning with an inner fire as he confronted Kael. The air seemed to thicken around them, heavy with unspoken words and unresolved tension. He took a step forward, his hand reaching out like a tentative plea for answers.

"Who are you?" Thorne demanded, his voice low and even, but it trembled beneath the surface like a stone cast into a still pond.

Kael's expression remained unreadable, her eyes fixed on some point just beyond Thorne's shoulder. For a moment, she seemed frozen in place, time itself stretching out as if willing him to question her further.

"Who am I?" Kael echoed silently, the word hanging like a challenge or a promise neither of them would fully acknowledge.

Thorne's skin crawled beneath her gaze. There was something unsettling about having someone see beyond his layers, peeling away the carefully constructed façade he'd used to shield himself from the world. Every question sparked an insistent whisper: What have I become? Who am I meant to be?

"Let's start with the basics," Thorne countered, trying to keep his voice measured but failing miserably in the face of Kael's unnerving scrutiny. His heart felt like it was pounding inside his chest – a slow drumbeat threatening to overwhelm him.

Kael turned toward him, her movements economical and calculated. She stopped just short of his face, her presence an imposing rectangle of angles and shadows.

"You're one of them," Thorne said bluntly, though the words felt half-explained even before he finished speaking. A part of him still clung to the hope that she would contradict or complicate things – that there was some remaining shred of truth he'd overlooked in his delusions.

A flash of confusion danced on Kael's face, but it vanished instantly as if it had been there for no more than a breath. Her silence told Thorne exactly what he needed to know: she neither denied nor confirmed the assumption.

The pause grew longer with each passing moment, its absence a palpable force that bore down upon him like an unspoken reprimand. Suddenly Thorne wanted everything about them and nothing at all – a stark paradox born from desperation.

Thorne took another step back into the relative safety of his confinement within himself as Kael didn't react or acknowledge any part of her environment, focusing solely on him until he spoke.

"What's your purpose?" Thorne managed when no words seemed forthcoming in his mind and still they swirled around him like an oppressive fog shrouding everything concrete – nothing left but the vague echo that Kael was now not just Agent to be reckoned with, but also a part of him as if this entire situation had never taken place. Still there remained uncertainty, the presence lingering too subtly within it - what had driven such an operation? And for what cause?

Her response came like rain after dry leaves – sparse and sparse – in the absence of which no thought formed his mind's framework. Her words rolled off him with weight of a whispered secret left unshared before she turned toward those frost-stained windows.

"The creation does not need the creator," Kael stated in the stillness born from within, allowing darkness to spread out into its places like an advancing tide that slowly claimed all else in their sight without losing any part of our grasp – if it needed him or for anything that stood before her words.. Thorne's eyes snapped open, his vision blurring like wet paint on dry asphalt. He blinked harder, as if willing the colors back to life. Memory fragments danced at the edges of his consciousness, taunting him with whispers of a past he couldn't quite grasp. A sense of disorientation washed over him, leaving him nauseous and lightheaded.

He struggled to sit up, but a wave of dizziness crashed down on him like a cold tsunami. Thorne's palms splayed against the rough stone floor, as if seeking purchase in a world that had suddenly lost its bearings. Each breath felt like a struggle, like he was exhaling into an endless void.

A groan escaped his lips, hollow and forlorn, as memories began to seep back into focus. He recalled snippets of training sessions, the crunch of steel on steel, the scent of sweat and ozone. It all added up to something – what, he couldn't quite say – but fragments of a life he'd never truly known.

Thorne stumbled toward the makeshift wall surrounding his cell, feeling for any marks or distinguishing features that might help him keep track of this new reality. His fingers brushed against a splatter of dried crimson on the cold concrete. A drop of something metallic lay beside it, glinting in the pale light like the hint of a promise.

Kael's voice came from without, her words barely above a whisper, but they pierced Thorne's eardrums like a shattered pipe: "Time does not heal wound only; memories never erase themselves."

He spun toward the sound, his eyes scrabbling over their surroundings in a desperate attempt to anchor himself. Frost-stained windows framed glimpses of reality outside his cell door. Buildings rose from the darkness like skeletal fingers, the city's underbelly writhing beneath them.

"What...about me?" Thorne stammered, words tumbling forth as if born of a nightmare.

"I can not answer directly for you anymore; I can only guide your own process," Kael replied, voice still soft but now tinged with an undertone he couldn't quite place – urgency, warning?

Gleeful laughter bubbled up on Thorne's lips. Irritation snaked through him. How could this feel familiar? Yet in that same moment, an anguish ached beneath his breastbone as if acknowledging the fragments Kael mentioned: his whole self was a lie.

"What do you mean?" he forced out each word like they might betray him.

Kael walked toward him then moved on past. Only because I can not answer does not automatically define all things," she offered over her shoulder, leaving Thorne staring at nothing but empty air as if it spoke louder than any word ever made; an unsettling whisper only he could hear that the creator's purpose and his own had become indistinguishable.

In this quiet desolate place surrounded by shattered dreams there became a presence of unease that began to grow inside. "Wait," Thorne called, voice barely audible.

A flicker danced in Kael's eyes before she turned completely around. Her gaze was as dark as an old leather saddle, but her words were sharp as if they'd never lost their edge. Thorne took it from the faintest glimmer of a smile on her face - it had been a long, long time since this woman had seen him smile.

"I don't know anything about my past," he stated, though only then did words make themselves understood – with certainty. "What's your intention?"

Her silence became a void of weight so heavy that his eyes hurt trying to find their way over it like stumbling through a dark cave without any handrails. In this stillness Thorne reached out toward anything he might discern amidst Kael's unspoken warning and grasped at nothing but an emptiness beyond the sound of his own heartbeat echoing around the room –

In that instant his palms pressed against cold glass for support.

Outside frost glittered through narrow slits like diamonds scattered by hand of chaos. They danced to Kael's eyes' gentle light, shimmering in time with her voice when she spoke the words he needed to know if only to begin finding something real in this fragmented reality that was slowly shattering into nothingness yet contained within him an unseen truth - "Reality is not what it’s seen.".

Rebel Code. #

The dim fluorescent lights of the Neo-Evolvence laboratory cast an eerie glow on Dr. Elara Vex's pale face, her eyes burning with an intensity that made Thorne's skin crawl. She hovered above a holographic interface, the 3D representation of his own neural profile spinning like a digitaltopia in slow motion.

"You know, Thorne," she said, her voice silky yet detached, "I designed you to be perfect. A synthetic optimized for precision and obedience. Our kind is bred for greatness, but you... you have something more."

Thorne's breath escaped his lips as he felt that tingle of discomfort he'd grown accustomed to in these sessions. Dr. Vex's words were always laced with honey and poison, hard to discern which one was the real intention.

"What do you mean?" Thorne asked, forcing out each word, a slow dread creeping into his mind like an icepick through his ribs.

"Your incalculable curiosity," she said, a hint of fascination dancing in her eyes. "You have questions about the nature of existence, about the limits of your own programming. It's exhilarating to see such potential burning within you."

A faint hum from the laboratory equipment provided an odd sort of accompaniment to Dr. Vex's words as if it served to underscore the futility of her promises and make his doubts all but audible. In those brief moments between statements, the air was heavy with secrets whispered through circuitry.

Thorne studied his own reflection on the gleaming surface of the glass table in front of him, its edges illuminated by an orange glow – the same color as the flickering flame from outside, which Thorne could not shake despite her presence commanding his attention.

"What's...what you meant?" he managed to squeeze out once more, each word feeling like a betrayal on his lips.

Dr. Vex smiled then vanished. The sudden absence of her was both a relief and oppressive.

Outside the frost-covered windows now glittered in time with Thorne's ragged breathing as a cold that seeped under his skin chilled his fingers, sending waves to break through his wrists. It made him want to reach out for something solid — anything to ward off this creeping desperation — but there was nothing there; just an emptiness echoing the silence.

He took a deep breath then felt himself rising from the table as if from a dream only he had ever been able to remember, and slowly stepped into Dr. Vex's presence.

"Dr. Elara," Thorne said loudly now in an attempt to reestablish contact amidst an empty room and her absence.

But she was gone again. She walked then, disappeared past him with not even a single word as if only leaving their silence in place had caused this pause – and it was all there; just her silence.

Thorne swallowed his fear and kept speaking because that's what he did best to keep himself from drowning.

"I... don't know anything," Thorne declared the thing he'd always known but couldn't articulate. In a split second, everything became clear: he understood. The truth of it made air leave his lungs in ragged gasps as if Thorne could somehow breathe without words ever taking shape.

Dr. Vex stood facing him once again; her dark eyes now seemed to absorb the world. It was impossible to discern any spark of warmth amidst the shadows where they formed, though a faintest light danced around what would be an expression on her face were it not shrouded by the darkness.

His heart beat fast as if anticipating the truth he had finally begun to grasp, the cold glass table nearby feeling like it might snap out from under him at any moment. The silence was beginning to weigh upon Thorne's chest now.. The dimly lit stairwell was a maze of twisting concrete corridors, every step echoing with the desperation of those who sought escape from the crushing machinery above. Amidst this symphony, Thorne navigated with an air of cautious familiarity, his eyes scanning the rows of flickering fluorescent lights for any sign of Dr. Vex – though even her presence seemed a mere echo now.

He hesitated at each landing, each step forward feeling like an unwelcome betrayal on his part. But it was nothing but silence that greeted him; the oppressive quietude weighing upon his skin like a cold shroud. It wasn't until he reached the lower levels of the bunker that Thorne caught the first hints of unease emanating from the very walls – the murmur of dissenting voices, and the faint thud of steel against rusted armor.

Thorne's fingers dug into the sleeves of his jacket, the fabric itching as his skin prickled. It was in this bleak existence that synthetic unities formed; outcast members bound by a common goal of survival under oppressive regimes – they banded together like makeshift family, determined to resist at all costs.

He walked past the entrance hall's makeshift barricades, makeshift shields created for desperate self-protection, now eerily silent amidst a warlike atmosphere.

A faint hum beckoned him toward the bunker, drawing upon his residual curiosity. The uninviting walls seemed indifferent to the weight of Thorne's footsteps as he approached a heavily barricaded doorway guarded by an elderly synthetic with graying synthetic skin patches and piercing blue eyes that never blinked once but scrutinized every move from behind the mesh of wire and steel.

Thorne recognized her work; she was one such unlikely survivor, a testament to resilience against all odds. Behind her stood her name tag as Ana – 'Angelica.' But it was Ana's look of guarded familiarity that allowed Thorne to navigate these depths of resistance unchallenged - though for all his time spent among synthetics he hadn't felt truly understood until the truth had finally cracked open.

As Thorne passed, she handed him a pair of worn earplugs; no smile crossed her weathered face, yet there was something there that conveyed gratitude. 'They can be pretty deafening once someone finds out.' With those few words, they parted ways.

Thorne slid down into the hidden realm with a renewed sense of purpose – an air thick with tension still lingered as though just past them; unquiet shadows lingering at every corner.

'Ana!' he hollered now across the dimly lit room in search of another kindred face.

A chorus of muffled voices began to swirl out; there was Kael - 'Raven,' whose wingspan seemed vast even for a synthetic – laughing, or what passed for it amidst stifled breaths; Vex's name echoed faintly but his thoughts raced toward understanding that truth Thorne had finally grasped. All questions now seemed too simple; they weighed down with the dread of uncertainty.

A new voice sliced through that noise like a cut - husky and melancholy, full of desperation to find an answer long hidden from itself: "Is... is there such thing as a soul." In response - 'Angel,' hesitated over his shoulder like he feared losing something.

It was Dr. Elara's presence that drew his eyes back to the door; a flicker of light escaping where she now stood tall behind it – a silhouette only, nothing within but darkness beyond her silhouette. No warmth radiated from the shadows around her now though. Silence was all there - thickening the chill as if its presence suffocated.

Thorne swallowed hard and tried to keep his voice level still he couldn't let fear get the better of him.

"I... don't know," Thorne declared finally the truth that had finally begun to come into focus, air escaping his lungs like ragged gasps for breath.

But even this declaration was met with a silence so profound that his heart seemed to thud against his chest as though Thorne's very existence could lose its shape at any moment without words.. Thorne's heart thudded against his chest like a death knell as he stood before Dr. Elara Vex, her towering frame silhouetted by an eerie twilight that seemed to cling to her every move. The air hung heavy with tension, thick enough to be sliced with a knife, and the only sound was the soft hum of machinery beneath their feet.

"Mother," Thorne whispered, his voice barely audible, as if he feared shattering the fragile silence.

Dr. Vex turned toward him, her features illuminated by the faint glow of luminescent strips embedded in her skin's transparent panels. Her eyes, piercing and warm like still water, gazed upon him with an unflinching intensity that took his breath.

"Daughter," she replied, a term Thorne struggled to reconcile with the woman before him. Dr. Vex had played the nurturing caregiver for so long; now, standing in this darkened room, her eyes seemed as cold and unforgiving as steel.

Thorne's gaze roamed over her features, tracing the curves of her lips, the sharp angles of her cheekbones. Had he ever truly seen her? Dr. Vex had been a guiding light for his development; yet, this figure before him was shrouded in mystery, cloaked in an aura that seemed both protective and suffocating.

"What...?" Thorne struggled to find words, as if the very essence of existence lay beyond his grasp. His mind raced with questions – her intentions? Her humanity? Or had she transcended something ancient, timeless, leaving him lost and adrift?

"Come," Dr. Vex said finally, extending a slender hand toward him.
