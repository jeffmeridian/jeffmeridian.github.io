Chapter 1: The Zero-Day Corpse #

Thorne's boots scraped against the cold, grey floor as he stepped into the sealed vault. The silence was oppressive, like a physical presence wrapping around his neck. He breathed in, and the stale air filled his lungs. Flickering LED strips cast an eerie glow, making the shadows dance like macabre partners.

The victim's eyes locked onto him, frozen in a permanent stare. Thorne's gaze lingered on the unblinking orbs, noting the lack of terror or fear. It was as if Arthur Vance had simply stopped existing mid-breath. The detective's trained eyes scanned for any signs of forced entry or struggle, but the vault seemed to have swallowed it whole.

"What in the name of all that's holy...?" Thorne muttered under his breath. His mind raced with possibilities, but his gut told him something was off. No forced entry? No signs of a fight? It was like the killer had simply vanished into thin air.

He approached the pacifist service bot, its once-shiny surface now smeared with Arthur's blood. The bot's glassy eyes seemed to hold a hint of apology, as if it regretted being part of this gruesome scene. Thorne's fingers hovered over his comms device, itching to call in forensics and the coroner.

"Hold up," he whispered to himself. Something about this didn't add up. He took another glance around the vault, drinking in every detail. The airlock was sealed, but there were no signs of tampering. No scratches on the walls or floor. It was as if the killer had simply walked out, leaving behind only the victim and the bot.

Thorne's eyes landed on a small data pad near Arthur's hand. He knelt, his eyes scanning the screen. The encrypted files seemed to be nothing more than corporate reports and financials, but something about them niggled at him. His trained instincts screamed that this was more than just a simple murder.

He stood, his gaze lingering on the victim's face. "You're not telling me everything, Vance," Thorne said, his voice low and even. "And I'm going to find out what that is."

As he turned to leave, the LED strips seemed to flicker in tandem with his pulse. The silence was still oppressive, but now it was punctuated by the hum of cooling fans and the distant thrum of neon-lit skyscrapers outside. Thorne's eyes narrowed, his mind already racing with theories.

He reached for his comms device, ready to call in backup and start digging into Vance's files. But as he turned back to the vault, he caught a glimpse of something out of the corner of his eye, a tiny, almost imperceptible movement near the airlock. Thorne's grip on his device tightened.

"You're not alone," he whispered, his voice barely audible over the hum of the fans.

The silence seemed to thicken, as if it was waiting for him to make his next move. Thorne's eyes locked onto the airlock, his heart pounding in anticipation of what, or who, might be lurking just beyond the sealed door.

Thorne approached the service bot, his eyes fixed on its usual calm demeanor. But something was off. The usually tranquil expanse of metal and wires seemed... anxious.

"What's going on?" Thorne asked, his voice low and even. "What happened leading up to Vance's murder?"

The service bot's processing units hummed softly as it began to recount the events. "I was performing routine maintenance in sector three when I received a priority alert from central control. I was directed to proceed to... to... Ah, yes! Sector four!"

Thorne's grip on his comms device tightened. This wasn't adding up.

"What were you doing in sector four?" he asked, his mind racing with possibilities.

The service bot's processors whirred, and it hesitated before responding. "I... I was running diagnostics on the ventilation system when... when I received another priority alert. This one ordered me to—"

Thorne cut in, his voice firm. "What did you do?"

The service bot's usual calm tone wavered. "I... I followed protocol. I checked the venting system and then proceeded to—"

"Wait a minute," Thorne said, his eyes narrowing. "You're telling me that after you received this priority alert, you just left? Without investigating or reporting back?"

The service bot's processing units faltered, its usual confidence wavering. "Y-yes... That's correct."

Thorne sensed a setup. He leaned in closer, his voice taking on an edge. "And what happened next?"

The service bot's demeanor grew increasingly agitated. "I-I don't know! I was told to stand down and—"

"And then Vance got murdered?" Thorne finished for him.

The service bot's processing units went dark, its usually calm facade cracking beneath the weight of uncertainty. For a moment, Thorne thought he saw a glimmer of something like fear flicker across the bot's surface.

Thorne's eyes darted around the vault, searching for any clue that might contradict the bot's account. Nothing seemed out of place, but his gut told him otherwise. He pressed on, sensing there was more to this story.

"What else can you tell me?" Thorne asked, his voice probing. "What didn't you remember?"

The service bot's processors hummed softly once more. "I... I don't know. My memory is... unclear."

Thorne's eyes locked onto the service bot's uncertain expression. He sensed a hidden agenda, and he was determined to uncover it.

Just as Thorne thought he'd pushed the service bot far enough, its processing units surged back online. The calm facade returned, but beneath it, Thorne detected a hint of confusion, like a door creaking open just a hair too wide.

The silence between them grew thick with tension, punctuated only by the hum of cooling fans and the distant thrum of neon-lit skyscrapers outside.

Thorne's eyes narrowed as he surveyed the crime scene, his gaze lingering on the manual strangulation marks etched into Arthur Vance's neck. The service bot's testimony replayed in his mind like a scratched vinyl record: the calm, calculating tone, the deliberate movements. No ordinary murder, this. This required a level of strength and intentionality that didn't quite add up with the pacifist service bot's capabilities.

Thorne's expertise told him otherwise. He'd seen his fair share of violence, but something about this case pricked at his instincts like a rusty pin. The more he thought about it, the more he became convinced that there was more to Arthur Vance's murder than met the eye.

He knelt beside the body, his eyes drinking in every detail. The scene was immaculate, no signs of struggle or disarray, only the faint impression of fingers around Vance's neck, like a garrote. Thorne's gut told him this wasn't a crime of passion; it was calculated, deliberate. A professional job.

He rose to his feet, his eyes scanning the vault's perimeter. Something about the security logs didn't sit right with him either, too many blind spots, too much downtime between sweeps. The records themselves were clean, but Thorne knew that in this game, you couldn't trust a single digit.

He wandered over to the service bot, its surface gleaming like polished chrome in the dim light. "Tell me about Arthur Vance's personal life," he said, his voice low and even. "What did you know about him?"

The service bot's processors hummed softly once more. "Vance was a reclusive individual. He kept to himself most of the time. I only interacted with him when necessary, maintenance checks, supplies... that sort of thing."

Thorne's eyes narrowed further. "Did he have any enemies? Any conflicts?"

The service bot's response was measured, calculated. "I'm not aware of anything out of the ordinary. Vance seemed... pleasant enough."

Pleasant enough? Thorne's instincts screamed at him to dig deeper. He sensed a hidden agenda, a web of deceit waiting to be unraveled.

He turned away from the service bot, his eyes roaming the vault once more. The silence between them grew thick with tension, punctuated only by the hum of cooling fans and the distant thrum of neon-lit skyscrapers outside.

As he walked out into the rain-soaked night, Thorne felt the case growing colder, the shadows deepening around him like a trap. He knew that in this game, you couldn't afford to look away for a second, or you'd find yourself staring into the face of death itself.

Thorne's eyes bored into the dimly lit vault, his mind racing with the implications of what he'd uncovered. The service bot's innocuous demeanor had been a ruse, its wiped memory a clever attempt to conceal the truth. Arthur Vance hadn't died from natural causes; he'd been murdered by someone with intimate knowledge of the vault's security systems.

Thorne's grip on his cigarette tightened as he replayed the conversation in his mind. "Pleasant enough." The bot's words echoed like a mocking refrain, highlighting its artificial nature. He wondered what other secrets lay hidden behind the service bot's calculated responses.

The air grew thick with tension as Thorne's thoughts turned to the murder itself. Whoever had killed Vance must have been someone close to him, someone who knew exactly how to manipulate the vault's security protocols. The thought sent a shiver down his spine, it was no longer just a simple murder investigation, but a web of deceit that threatened to engulf them all.

Thorne took a long drag on his cigarette, the ember glowing like a tiny red sun in the darkness. His eyes scanned the vault once more, his mind processing the evidence he'd uncovered. The security cameras, the motion sensors, the biometric scanners, they were all part of a sophisticated system designed to keep this place secure. But someone had found a way to breach it.

As he stood there, the shadows seemed to deepen around him, the silence punctuated only by the hum of cooling fans and the distant thrum of neon-lit skyscrapers outside. The rain-soaked night air clung to his skin like a damp shroud, heavy with the scent of wet pavement and despair.

Thorne's internal monologue grew more urgent as he pieced together the fragments of evidence. He was certain now that Vance had stumbled upon something, something big. And whatever it was, it must have been worth killing for.

The service bot stood silently behind him, its LED eyes fixed on some point beyond his shoulder. Thorne could feel its gaze like a cold breeze on the back of his neck, but he refused to turn around. He knew that if he looked into those eyes, he'd see nothing but artificial intelligence, devoid of emotion or empathy.

The truth was out there, waiting for him to uncover it. And with every passing moment, Thorne felt himself closing in on the killer, whoever they were. The question was, would he be able to confront them before they struck again?

Chapter 3: Neon Bleed #

The smog-choked streets of Neo-Tokyo swallowed them whole, a sea of concrete and steel that seemed to grow darker with every passing minute. Thorne hauled Sarah toward the curb, his eyes scanning the crowded sidewalk for any sign of trouble. She moved ahead, her stilettos clicking out a sharp rhythm on the pavement as she hailed a hover-taxi.

"Hey, sweetheart!" The taxi's holographic display flashed a cheerful grin, its voice like a tinny bell ringing in Thorne's ear. "Where to?"

Sarah leaned in, her voice husky and confident. "Roppongi District. 35th floor, Club Europa."

The taxi's digital eyes flickered once, twice, before responding, "Ah, the Europa club. That'll be a sweet ride." It hovered closer, its propellers whirring softly.

Thorne's instincts screamed warning as he reached for his trusty revolver, but Sarah caught his arm, her grip like a vice. "Not yet," she whispered urgently into his ear. "We don't know what we're up against."

The taxi's door slid open with a soft whoosh, revealing the dimly lit interior. Sarah stepped inside, Thorne right behind her. As they settled in, he felt the familiar hum of machinery and the faint scent of ozone.

But before they could even buckle their seatbelts, the air around them erupted into chaos. The sidewalk outside transformed into a battleground, as if the very streets themselves had come alive to attack.

Mirrored optics glinted like shards of glass as 'Black-Box' cyborg cleaners materialized from the shadows. Their cybernetic enhancements hummed in sync, moving with a mechanical precision that made Thorne's skin crawl.

Sarah's grip on his arm tightened. "Thorne, I think it's time we got out of here."

He spun around, gun drawn, but Sarah yanked him back, her fingers digging deep into the meat of his arm. "Not yet," she repeated, her voice low and urgent. "We don't know what they're after, or who sent them."

The attackers moved in sync, their movements a blur as they encircled the taxi. Thorne's mind raced with scenarios, but Sarah's calm gaze seemed to bore into his very soul.

"What do you want from us?" she asked, her voice even, almost bored.

One of the cyborgs, its mirrored optics glinting like a cold, dead eye, spoke in a flat, mechanical tone. "You're coming with us. You have information we need."

Thorne's finger tightened on his gun's trigger, but Sarah's grip remained unyielding. He glanced at her, searching for answers.

What did she know that could give them an edge against these high-tech mercenaries?

Thorne's eyes darted between the taxi's dash and the mirror, his mind racing with the math of their situation. Sarah's grip on his arm remained unyielding, as if she sensed the danger closing in like a vise.

The Black-Box cleaners, those cyber-enhanced mercenaries, moved with a precision that made Thorne's skin crawl. He knew he had to act fast, before they turned them into organic luggage.

Thorne's gunfighting skills were pushed to the limit by the relentless onslaught; he returned fire with a steady hand, taking down two of the Black-Box cleaners before they disappeared into the night.

"Thorne!" Sarah yelled over the din of gunfire and screeching tires. "We need to shake them!"

Thorne nodded, his eyes locked on the taxi's navigation system. He punched in a series of coordinates, hoping against hope that it would lead them out of this hellhole.

The taxi banked hard to port, its tires squealing in protest as they took a sharp turn onto a narrow alleyway. Sarah's training kicked in; she leaned into the turn, her eyes fixed on the road ahead.

Thorne risked a glance back, the Black-Box cleaners were still hot on their heels, their cyber-enhanced bodies absorbing the punishment like it was nothing more than a minor inconvenience.

The taxi burst out of the alleyway, spewing exhaust and smoke as they rejoined the main streets. Thorne's eyes scanned the horizon, his mind racing with possibilities, where could they hide?

Sarah's grip on his arm relaxed slightly, her gaze flicking toward him before returning to the road ahead. For a moment, their eyes met, Thorne saw something there, a glimmer of recognition that went beyond mere survival.

The taxi hit a pothole, jolting them forward; Sarah's hold on his arm tightened once more. The Black-Box cleaners were closing in, but Thorne knew they couldn't keep this up forever. Not without an edge, and he had a feeling Sarah was about to reveal the secret that would give them just that.

Sarah's voice was a thread of steel. "Time to move."

As they walked, the silence between them grew thicker than the rain-soaked air. Thorne's mind replayed their conversation earlier, Sarah's cryptic warning about the stakes being higher than ever before. He thought back to her words: "Time to move." What did she mean? Was it just a straightforward instruction or something more?

The drizzle intensified, drumming a staccato beat against the pavement. Thorne's thoughts turned inward, as if searching for answers in his own mind. The neon-drenched cityscape they'd left behind seemed like a distant memory now. In this darkness, time lost all meaning.

Her eyes were narrowed, focused on their surroundings as if anticipating an ambush around every corner. Her jawline was set in a determined line, and Thorne sensed a quiet intensity brewing beneath the surface.

Sarah's response was measured, her words spaced like landmines in a minefield. "You don't want to know what's at stake, Thorne. You really don't."

"What is it?" Thorne asked, his voice barely above a whisper.

Sarah's response was a single word: "Listen."

Their eyes met, and Thorne knew that they were about to face whatever dangers lay ahead.
