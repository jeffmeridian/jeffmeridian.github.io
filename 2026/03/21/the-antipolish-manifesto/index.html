Title: The Anti Polish Manifesto

Author: jeff meridian

🚀Write bad.
🔥 Spill the chaos.
💥Embrace the typos.
💡Never polish.

The Anti‑Polish Manifesto: Why Embracing Chaos Might Be Your Best Writing Strategy

When the publishing world tells you to “polish until it shines,” In an era where algorithms sniff out perfection, the most rebellious act you can commit is to celebrate typos, and to publish the first draft before AI has a chance to brand you as robot.

For decades we’ve been sold a fairy‑tale: a writer drafts, revises, edits, beta‑reads, proof‑reads, and finally releases a pristine product. The reality? That pipeline is a pipeline for algorithms to flag your work as “AI‑generated” or “over‑engineered.” When every sentence is trimmed to a razor’s edge, you lose the very thing that makes you human—imperfection.

There’s a strange alchemy in raw text. A stray “teh” or an unfinished thought is a breadcrumb that leads readers deeper into the writer’s mind. Typos are fingerprints. They prove a living hand wrote the words.

Fragmented sentences spark curiosity. Readers fill the gaps, engaging more actively.
Inconsistent punctuation mirrors real speech. It creates intimacy, a conversational tone that polished prose often lacks.

Publish the First Draft—Fast. The moment you hit “publish,” the chaos is locked in time, immune to the temptation of subsequent AI “clean‑ups.”

If a perfectly polished manuscript is flagged as “AI‑generated,” it may be relegated to a legal gray zone, or worst of all, publishing jail. By intentionally preserving the messy edges of your work, you stake a claim to pure human authorship. Human‑only clauses in contracts are gaining traction. They reward writers who can prove their work isn’t machine‑refined.
Publish the raw, the rough, the real. Let your mistakes be the banner under which you march.

Chaos doesn’t mean abandonment of structure entirely. Think of it as a controlled disorder:

The goal is to create a living document, a snapshot of thoughts in motion, rather than a static, sterile product.

“Publish with your mistakes, and be proud of it.”
